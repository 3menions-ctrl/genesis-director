Seedance 2.0 is now live in Small Bridges, and it changes what a single generated shot can look like. If you've ever watched an AI clip and felt the motion was *almost* right — a little floaty, a little plastic — Seedance is the engine built to close that gap.

Here's what's new, when to use it, and how it sits alongside the other engines in the studio.

## What "hyperreal motion" actually means

Most video engines are very good at the first frame. The hard part is everything after it: how cloth settles, how weight shifts through a step, how light wraps a face as it turns. Seedance 2.0 is tuned specifically for that in-between — the physics of motion rather than the prettiness of a still.

In practice you'll notice three things:

- **Weight.** Characters move like they have mass. A jump lands. A coat swings and stops.
- **Micro-detail.** Skin, hair and fabric hold up under slow motion instead of smearing.
- **Camera honesty.** Push-ins and pans feel shot, not slid — with real parallax between foreground and background.

## When to reach for Seedance

Seedance is a premium engine, so use it where motion *is* the story:

- Hero shots and title moments
- Slow-motion beauty passes (product reveals, athletes, food)
- Close-ups where a face has to carry emotion
- Any shot a viewer will pause on

For coverage, b-roll, and quick social cuts, the lighter engines are faster and cheaper — and the difference won't show at scroll speed.

## How it compares to the rest of the room

The studio gives you five engines so you can match the tool to the shot:

- **Wan 2.5** — the free tier. Great for drafts, ideation, and high-volume coverage.
- **Kling V3** — cinematic with native lip-sync; your default for dialogue.
- **Seedance 2.0** — premium hyperreal motion; the new flagship for hero shots.
- **Veo 3 Fast** — native audio at 1080p, quick turnarounds.
- **Sora 2** — narrative coherence over longer shots and complex scenes.

You don't have to commit to one. A finished film on Small Bridges routinely mixes engines shot to shot — a Sora establishing wide, Kling dialogue in the middle, a Seedance close-up for the emotional beat.

## Getting the most out of it

A few prompting notes that help Seedance shine:

1. **Describe the motion, not just the subject.** "She turns slowly, hair catching the light" beats "a woman."
2. **Give it a camera move.** Seedance rewards intent — "slow dolly in," "handheld follow."
3. **Keep shots short and specific.** One clear action per clip cuts cleaner and renders truer.

Then drop the clip onto the timeline, restitch if you want a different take, and let the studio score and cut around it.
