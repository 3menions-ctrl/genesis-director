Generating one good clip is a solved problem. The hard part — the part that separates a finished film from a folder of clips — is making *many* shots feel like the same story. Same character. Same world. Same look. Cut together with intent.

This is where most AI video falls apart, and it's exactly what the Small Bridges timeline is built to hold together. Here's the workflow for multi-scene films.

## Start with structure, not shots

Before you generate anything, write the beats. Even three lines is enough:

1. A figure wakes in a fog-drowned manor.
2. They cross a moonlit courtyard.
3. They step through a tear in reality.

Now you're shooting *toward* something. Each beat becomes one or more clips, and you already know how they connect.

## Lock your cast

The fastest way to break a film is a character whose face changes between cuts. Avoid it by casting from the avatar catalog rather than re-rolling a generic person each shot. Your lead is the *same* avatar in scene one and scene nine.

**Continuity lock** carries that identity — and the overall style — across cuts, so the person who wakes in the manor is unmistakably the person who steps through the rift.

## Keep the world coherent

Pick environments that share a palette and time of day. The catalog's 120 worlds carry their own lighting and color grade, so choosing "moonlit courtyard" and "candlelit hall" gives you scenes that already feel like the same night. Jarring tone shifts read as mistakes; matched grades read as direction.

## Choose engines per shot — on purpose

A film doesn't need one engine; it needs the *right* engine per shot:

- **Sora 2** for the establishing wide and any long, complex move.
- **Kling V3** for dialogue — native lip-sync keeps mouths honest.
- **Seedance 2.0** for the emotional close-up you want viewers to feel.
- **Wan 2.5** for connective coverage and quick alternates.

Mixing engines is normal. The timeline blends them into one cut.

## Restitch instead of restarting

Got a scene that's 90% right but one shot lands wrong? You don't rebuild the film. **Restitch** swaps that single clip — a new take, a different engine, a tighter action — while the rest of the cut, the timing and the score stay intact. This is the feature that makes iteration cheap, and cheap iteration is what makes films good.

## Let the studio cut and score around you

Once your shots are on the timeline, the studio handles the connective tissue: transitions that match your pacing, a score that follows the mood, sound design under the cuts. You direct the shots; it assembles the film.

## Then export the whole thing

When the cut holds together, export at up to 4K in any aspect ratio. What started as three lines of story is now a finished, continuous film — not a pile of clips.

## The takeaway

Consistency isn't luck; it's a workflow. Cast once, lock continuity, match your worlds, pick engines per shot, and restitch instead of restarting. Do that and your multi-scene film will feel like one story — because it is.
