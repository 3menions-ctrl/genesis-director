Every short-form platform runs on the same fuel: consistency. Post daily and the algorithm learns you; go quiet for a week and you start over. The problem is that "post daily" usually means film daily — set up, shoot, edit, repeat — and that pace breaks most creators within a month.

AI video changes the math. Here's how creators use Small Bridges to keep a daily cadence without the camera, the lighting, or the burnout.

## Batch the ideas, not the shoots

Instead of one idea per filming session, write ten prompts in one sitting. Because each clip starts as a sentence, your "shoot day" becomes a *writing* session — and you can generate a week of content in an afternoon.

Keep a running notes file of hooks. When inspiration hits, you describe; the studio shoots.

## Build a recurring character

The accounts that grow fastest have a *face* you recognize. Cast one of 534 avatars as your channel's recurring presenter and reuse them across every video. They look the same in every clip, speak in a consistent voice, and never need to be available for a shoot.

Continuity lock keeps that character on-model across cuts, so your series feels like a series — not a pile of unrelated clips.

## Lead with a hook that breaks the frame

Short-form lives or dies in the first second. The 4th-wall templates are built exactly for that: your presenter bulges the screen, grabs the bezel, and pulls themselves out of the feed — a literal pattern-interrupt that stops the scroll.

Start from a template like the viral hook or scroll-grab and you inherit the timing that already works.

## Score it without thinking about music

Sound is half of short-form. The studio auto-scores each cut with a music bed matched to the mood — trap, lofi, EDM, cinematic — and lays in sound effects. You can swap the vibe in a click, but you never start from silence.

## Cut native to the feed

Everything exports 9:16 with captions baked in. Pick a template's pacing — manic, fast, medium — and the cut matches how people actually watch: quick, caption-first, no dead air.

## A realistic daily workflow

Here's a cadence creators actually sustain:

1. **Sunday:** write 7–10 prompts around this week's theme.
2. **Generate:** queue the clips, pick your engine per shot (Wan for volume, Kling for talking).
3. **Assemble:** drop each into a template, restitch any take you don't love.
4. **Schedule:** export 9:16, post one a day.

That's a full week of content from one writing session — and you never touched a camera.

## Consistency without the cost

Burnout comes from the *production*, not the posting. Take the production out and daily becomes easy. Write the idea, cast your character, let the studio shoot and score it, and show up in the feed every single day.
